## Top picks

### 1. [Claude Mythos Preview](https://red.anthropic.com/2026/mythos-preview/)
**Source:** Anthropic (announcement) · 2026-05-05 · also covered by [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/05/anthropic-ceo-cyber-moment-of-danger-mythos-vulnerabilities.html)
Anthropic announced Mythos Preview, a frontier model positioned for autonomous offensive vulnerability research. Claims include rediscovery of a 17-year-old FreeBSD NFS RCE (CVE-2026-4747), ~300 vulnerabilities found in Firefox, and zero-days across major OSes/browsers from prompts as bare as "find a security vulnerability in this program." Initial access is gated through "Project Glasswing" for ~40 critical-software organizations. CNBC's coverage adds claims of an OpenBSD bug from 27 years ago and a 16-year-old FFmpeg flaw.
**Methodology:** frontier LLM driven by minimal natural-language prompts to perform end-to-end vuln discovery and exploitation, with restricted preview deployment.
**Why it matters:** if even half the claims hold up, this is the clearest signal yet that agentic vuln discovery has moved from benchmark wins to mass-finding real bugs in mature codebases — directly relevant to how AI4Sec evaluation should evolve.

### 2. [FunFuzz: An LLM-Powered Evolutionary Fuzzing Framework](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.02789)
**Source:** Mario Rodríguez Béjar, Bernardino Romera-Paredes, Jose Luis Hernández-Ramos · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-04 *(date uncertain)*
A multi-island evolutionary fuzzer in which each island runs an isolated, prompt-conditioned LLM search and high-value candidates migrate between islands. Initial-generation prompts are derived from documentation, islands are seeded with topic-specific instructions, and prompts adapt via feedback-guided selection. On 24-hour campaigns against GCC and Clang, FunFuzz reports higher coverage and more unique failures than prior LLM-driven fuzzing baselines.
**Methodology:** multi-island evolutionary algorithm with LLM-driven prompt generation, coverage-guided selection, and internal failure-signal scoring.
**Why it matters:** combines the two most-promising directions in fuzzing-with-LLMs (population-based search + prompt-conditioned generation) and reports against compiler-grade targets — a useful baseline if you build PoC-generation pipelines.

### 3. [Redefining AI Red Teaming in the Agentic Era: From Weeks to Hours](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04019)
**Source:** Raja Sekhar Rao Dheekonda, Will Pearce, Nick Landers · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-05
Operators currently hand-craft red-team workflows over weeks. The paper introduces an agent built on the open-source Dreadnode SDK that, from natural-language objectives, autonomously orchestrates over 45 adversarial attacks, 450+ transforms, and 130+ scorers, reporting 85% attack success against Meta Llama Scout. Framed as a programmable agentic harness rather than a single attack technique.
**Methodology:** agentic orchestrator over a library of attacks/transforms/scorers, driven by natural-language objectives.
**Why it matters:** a concrete, library-grounded reference for building automated red-team agents — useful both as a baseline to beat and as a tool to repurpose for your own evaluation runs.

### 4. [MultiBreak: A Scalable and Diverse Multi-turn Jailbreak Benchmark for Evaluating LLM Safety](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.01687)
**Source:** arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-03
A multi-turn jailbreak benchmark with 10,389 adversarial prompts spanning 2,665 distinct harmful intents. The paper argues multi-turn formats mimic natural conversation, are easier to bypass safety alignment than single-turn jailbreaks, and expose evaluation blind spots in current safety suites.
**Methodology:** curated benchmark generation pipeline over multi-turn conversational templates with intent taxonomy.
**Why it matters:** likely to become a default reference benchmark for multi-turn agent safety; worth pinning as a baseline if you publish on agent defenses.

### 5. [A Validated Prompt Bank for Malicious Code Generation: Separating Executable Weapons from Security Knowledge in 1,554 Consensus-Labeled Prompts](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03179)
**Source:** Richard J. Young, Gregory D. Moody · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-04
Existing benchmarks conflate "produce executable malicious code" with "explain a security concept," even though models likely route those through different refusal pathways. This work consolidates four source benchmarks and uses multi-judge consensus labeling to produce 1,554 prompts cleanly partitioned into the two classes.
**Methodology:** multi-judge consensus labeling pipeline over four consolidated source benchmarks.
**Why it matters:** lets you measure refusal behavior without the executable-vs-pedagogical confound; directly useful if you evaluate models on offensive-code tasks.

### 6. [When Embedding-Based Defenses Fail: Rethinking Safety in LLM-Based Multi-Agent Systems](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.01133)
**Source:** Lingxi Zhang, Guangtao Zheng, Hanjie Chen · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-01
Studies how malicious agents in an LLM multi-agent system propagate misinformation when defenders use embedding-distance pruning. The authors show — theoretically and empirically — that attackers can craft messages whose embeddings hug benign clusters, demonstrating the failure mode with three attacks: Slow Drift, Benign Wrapper, and Chaos Seeding.
**Methodology:** theoretical analysis + three crafted attacks against embedding-distance defenses in MAS.
**Why it matters:** if your multi-agent threat model leans on embedding-based isolation, this paper is the go-to reference for why that is brittle.

### 7. [How Code Representation Shapes False-Positive Dynamics in Cross-Language LLM Vulnerability Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27714)
**Source:** Maofei Chen, Laifu Wang, Yue Qin, Yuan Wang, Bo Wu, Dongxin Liu (China Telecom Research Institute) · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-04-30
Systematic study of how code representation (raw source vs. pruned ASTs) at training and at inference affects false-positive behavior when LLMs trained on C/C++ vulnerabilities are applied to Java and Python. Two 8B models (Qwen3-8B, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) are fine-tuned on Juliet and tested on OWASP and Python suites.
**Methodology:** controlled fine-tuning + inference experiments varying code-representation format across two 8B LLMs and three languages.
**Why it matters:** an honest look at a perennial problem with LLM vuln detectors (FP rates collapse cross-language); methodologically clean and easy to integrate into evaluation.

### 8. [Stable Agentic Control: Tool-Mediated LLM Architecture for Autonomous Cyber Defense](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03034)
**Source:** Kerri Prinos, Lilianne Brush, Cameron Denton, Zhanqi Wang, Joshua Knox, Snehal Antani, Anton Foltz, Amy Villaseñor · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-04 *(date uncertain)*
Targets EDR-policy decisions in a SOC under adversarial pressure. The architecture constrains LLM agents to call deterministic tools (Stackelberg best-response, a Bayesian observer, attack-graph primitives) and pick from finite action catalogs at the tool-output interface. A Lyapunov function machine-checked in Lean 4 certifies controllability, observability, and ISS robustness.
**Methodology:** LLM agent restricted to a deterministic tool catalog with Lean-4-verified stability properties.
**Why it matters:** rare example of formal-methods-style stability guarantees being grafted onto an LLM agent for a concrete defensive task; useful template if you care about provable agent behavior.

## Also notable

### [Latent Adversarial Detection: Adaptive Probing of LLM Activations for Multi-Turn Attack Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.28129)
**Prashant Kulkarni · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-04-30.** Trains residual-stream probes across four 24B–70B model families to spot the trust-build → pivot → escalate trajectory of multi-turn prompt injection. Lifts conversation-level detection from 76.2% to 93.8% on a synthetic suite; probes are model-specific. Useful counterpoint to text-level injection defenses.

### [A Sentence Relation-Based Approach to Sanitizing Malicious Instructions (SONAR)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.01078)
**Soumil Datta, Melissa Umble, Daniel S. Brown, Guanhong Tao · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-01.** Builds a sentence-level relational graph using NLI entailment/contradiction scores as edge weights, then prunes sentences that deviate from the user's core task before they reach the LLM. Targets RAG and tool-using agent settings.

### [PIIGuard: Mitigating PII Harvesting under Adversarial Sanitization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03129)
**Mingshuo Liu, Yiwei Zha, Min Chen · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-04.** Repurposes indirect prompt injection as a defensive primitive: page owners embed optimized hidden HTML fragments that steer browsing-enabled LLMs away from PII disclosure. Search uses rule-based leakage scoring, evolutionary mutation, and judge-based recoverability checks.

### [MAGE: Safeguarding LLM Agents against Long-Horizon Threats via Shadow Memory](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03228)
**arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-05 *(date uncertain)*.** Adds a dedicated safety-focused "shadow memory" alongside an agent's working memory to assess risk of pending actions across long-running interactions. Targets attacks that only become recognizable when state from many earlier turns is integrated.

### [Enhancing Agent Safety Judgment via Controlled Benchmark Rewriting (ROME)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03242)
**arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-05 *(date uncertain)*.** ROME is a multi-agent red-team pipeline that rewrites known unsafe trajectories into more deceptive evaluation instances, producing 300 challenge cases across contextual ambiguity, implicit risks, and shortcut-decision pitfalls. Pairs the benchmark with an analogical-reasoning safety judge.

### [When Agents Handle Secrets: A Survey of Confidential Computing for Agentic AI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03213)
**Javad Forough et al. · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-04.** Maps TEE platforms (Intel SGX/TDX, AMD SEV-SNP, ARM TrustZone/CCA, NVIDIA H100 CC) onto a perception/planning/memory/action/coordination threat model for agents. Lists six open challenges including compound attestation across multi-hop agent chains.

### [Dependency-Aware Privacy for Multi-turn Agents (RootGuard)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03188)
**arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-04.** Models adversaries that combine releases across turns to recover private attributes. RootGuard sanitizes "root" values once and derives subsequent releases deterministically, so end-to-end privacy depends only on the initial sanitization step.

### [Strengthening Secure Software at Global Scale: How MSRC is Evolving with AI](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/msrc/blog/2026/04/strengthening-secure-software-global-scale-how-msrc-is-evolving-with-ai)
**Microsoft MSRC blog · 2026-04-30 *(date uncertain)*.** Describes adding agentic red-teaming inside the MSDL, automating triage and remediation, and granting Project Glasswing customers preview access to Claude Mythos through Microsoft Foundry. References CTI-REALM as the benchmark for evaluating agents on real detection-engineering tasks.

### [A Low-Latency Fraud Detection Layer for Detecting Adversarial Interaction Patterns in LLM-Powered Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.01143)
**Sheldon Yu, Yingcheng Sun, Hanqing Guo, Julian McAuley, Qianqian Tong · arXiv cs.CR · 2026-05-01 *(date uncertain)*.** A runtime feature pipeline (prompt characteristics, session dynamics, tool-usage patterns, fraud-inspired signals) feeds a low-latency classifier that scores risk over interaction trajectories — pitched as a complement to prompt-level filters and rule-based guardrails.
